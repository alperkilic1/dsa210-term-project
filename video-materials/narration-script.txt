SLIDE 1 - TITLE

Hi, I am Alper Kilic, and this is my DSA210 term project: Incident Genome.

----------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 2 - THE PROBLEM

Cloud outages cost companies thousands of dollars per minute. My research
question is simple: can we predict whether an outage will be short or long,
using only what we can see in the first hour?

----------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 3 - THE DATA

I collected data from 14 public cloud status pages, including GitHub,
Cloudflare, Discord, and OpenAI. These services use Statuspage.io, which
exposes incident history as structured JSON. No API key is needed, so the
project is fully reproducible. After cleaning, I had 704 incidents. The target
variable is binary: short means under 60 minutes, long means 60 minutes or
more. The split is about 39 percent short and 61 percent long.

----------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 4 - EXPLORATORY ANALYSIS

In exploratory analysis, I found that median duration is 82.5 minutes with
heavy right skew. Incidents peak on Tuesdays around 3 PM UTC. Resolution speed
varies a lot across services -- Netlify resolves about five times faster than
Twilio.

----------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 5 - THE KEY FINDING

The most important finding was a data leakage problem. The original feature,
total number of updates, had a positive correlation of 0.46 with duration. But
that is because longer incidents naturally get more updates after they end.
When I replaced it with first-hour updates only, the correlation flipped to
negative 0.224. This sign flip proved the original correlation was an
artifact. Catching this leakage was critical for building an honest model.

----------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 6 - THE MODEL

For the ML model, I used a Random Forest classifier with five features:
service name, hour, weekday, first-hour update count, and a business-hours
flag. I handled class imbalance with balanced class weights and used
stratified splits.

----------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 7 - RESULTS

The Random Forest achieved 74.5 percent accuracy and an F1-macro score of
0.7317. The majority-class baseline only gets 61.7 percent accuracy and 0.3816
F1-macro. A binomial test confirms this improvement is statistically
significant, with a p-value of 6.67 times 10 to the negative 4.

----------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 8 - INTERPRETABILITY

SHAP analysis shows that first-hour updates dominates all other features by a
factor of five to six. Early communication intensity is the strongest
predictor of outage duration.

----------------------------------------------------------------------

SLIDE 9 - CONCLUSION

To conclude: yes, early signals do predict outage duration above chance. But
the effect size is small -- Cohen's h is 0.284 -- so this is not
production-ready yet. Future work includes NLP on update text and expanding to
more services. Thank you.
